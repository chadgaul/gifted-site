How Much Should You Spend on Christmas Gifts Per Person?

There's no single right number - but there are sensible ranges, and a method that keeps the season from quietly costing double what you planned.

Start with the total, not the person

The most common budgeting mistake is deciding gift-by-gift. Each purchase feels reasonable on its own, and the total only reveals itself on the January credit card statement. Flip it: pick a total holiday number first, subtract fixed costs (travel, hosting, decorations, cards), and divide the remainder among your people.

A useful anchor: financial planners commonly suggest keeping total holiday gift spending around 1–1.5% of your annual income. On a $70,000 income that's roughly $700–$1,050 for the whole season - a number that keeps Christmas generous without borrowing from the new year.

Typical ranges by relationship

Surveys of holiday spending land in fairly consistent bands. Adjust for your income and family culture, but as a starting point:

  • Spouse or partner: $100–$300
  • Your kids: $75–$200 each (many families set one equal number per child)
  • Parents and siblings: $50–$100
  • Nieces, nephews, cousins: $25–$50
  • Close friends: $25–$50
  • Coworkers, teachers, neighbors: $10–$30

Two principles matter more than the exact numbers. First, consistency beats size - a thoughtful $30 gift chosen from something they mentioned in June beats a panicked $80 gift card. Second, decide the number before you shop, because a limit set in the store isn't a limit.

Make the budget visible while you shop

A budget only works if you can see it at the moment of purchase. This is where most systems fail - the number lives in a spreadsheet at home while you're standing in a store (or in a checkout flow) at 10pm.

When budgets are uneven (and that's fine)

Equal spending isn't the goal - intentional spending is. It's normal to spend more on your kids than your coworkers, or more on a parent having a hard year. What causes stress and resentment is unplanned inequality you discover afterward. Setting per-person numbers up front, even rough ones, turns those choices into decisions instead of accidents.

The quiet budget-killers

  • Stocking stuffers - five "little things" at $12 each is $60 that never got counted. Track them like gifts.
  • Shipping and wrapping - either build them into the total or add them to gift prices.
  • Self-gifting - the "one for them, one for me" December phenomenon is real. If you do it, budget it.
  • Late additions - the forgotten neighbor on December 23rd. Keep a small "surprise people" buffer in your event budget.
